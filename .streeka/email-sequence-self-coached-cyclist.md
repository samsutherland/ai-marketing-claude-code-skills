## Email 2 — Day 2

**Subject:** Most training plans fail by design

---

The week your training falls apart, most people blame themselves.

They weren't disciplined enough. They let the week get away from them. They should have got up earlier.

Usually that's wrong.

Most plans are written for a hypothetical week — consistent sleep, no travel, no late meetings, a body that cooperates on demand. When something changes, the plan doesn't flex. It just sits there, already two sessions behind, waiting for you to catch up.

Three patterns I see constantly:

**The catch-up spiral.** Miss a session, try to fit it into tomorrow. Tomorrow's session gets compressed. You're tired going into the one session that actually mattered this week.

**The guilt skip.** Miss enough sessions that the gap feels too big to close. Stop opening the training app. Call the block a write-off.

**The hero effort.** Try to pack a full week into three days. Hit a wall. Need four days to recover.

None of these are discipline problems. They're what happens when the plan can't adapt.

The playbook covers the manual version — how to think through it yourself when the week goes sideways. Next email I'll show you how Tim used the same thinking when he missed a full week sick during his backyard ultra build-up.

— Sam

## Email 3 — Day 4

**Subject:** Tim missed a full week of training. Here's what he did.

---

Tim was training for a backyard ultra — the kind of event where you run 6.7km every hour until everyone else stops. Consistency in training matters more than almost anything.

Then he got sick. Lost the whole week.

He told me what he'd normally do: either cram the missed sessions back in, or pretend the week didn't happen and pick up where he left off. Both are bad options. The first digs a deeper hole. The second leaves you undertrained and anxious going into the next block.

Instead, he told SAMI what was going on. That he'd been sick but was feeling better. SAMI rebuilt the week around a sensible return — lighter structure, clear sessions, treadmill pace guidance so he knew exactly what effort level made sense coming back.

In Tim's words: *"I came back without guessing, panicking, or overdoing it."*

That's the thing a good coach does. Not rescuing the lost week — that's gone. Working out the best path forward from where you actually are.

Tim finished his backyard ultra.

— Sam

## Email 4 — Day 7

**Subject:** Why I built Streeka

---

I ate my way through the pandemic lockdowns and ended up at 101 kilos in January 2023.

I signed up for Peaks Challenge — the biggest bike race I could find — hired a coach, and started structured training for the first time in my life. Nine months later I finished in 12 hours 20 minutes.

Then winter came. I stopped riding. Put weight back on.

Trained for the same race the following year, this time without a coach. Used what I'd learned from the first time round. Finished in 10 hours.

The difference between the two attempts wasn't just fitness. The first time, I had someone who knew what to do when the week went wrong. When I was tired, or busy, or sick — the coach adjusted. I didn't have to figure it out.

The second time I had to figure it out myself. I had the knowledge from a year of being coached. Most people don't.

I spent a long time looking for an app that did what a coach does in those moments — not plans and tracking, but the actual thinking when things fall apart. I couldn't find one.

I was unhappy with my job and trying to work out what I actually wanted to do. When I got made redundant I built it.

That's Streeka. What a coach gives you when the week goes sideways — without hiring one.

— Sam
