## Email 2 — Day 3

**Subject:** The most useful question you can ask the AI

---

Most cyclists use Claude like a search engine.

They ask it for training advice. It gives them training advice that could apply to anyone. They decide AI isn't that useful for cycling.

One thing changes this.

Instead of asking the AI what to do, ask it what it can't know.

> "Based only on my Strava data, what can you confidently tell me about my training? What can you only guess? What important coaching context is missing?"

That question produces the most useful answers I've seen from Claude with Strava.

Because the AI will tell you exactly where the gap is. And the gap is almost always the same: it knows what you did, but not why you did it, what you were trying to do, or how it felt.

That's the coaching problem. Knowing where it lives is useful.

— Sam

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** What Tim asked Streeka when he came back from being sick

---

Tim was training for a backyard ultra — the kind of event where you run 6.7km every hour until everyone else stops.

He got sick. Lost a full week.

Normally he'd have opened his training plan, seen how far behind he was, and either crammed the sessions back in or called the block a write-off.

Instead he told SAMI — Streeka's AI — what had happened. That he'd been sick, was feeling better, had six weeks left before the event.

SAMI already knew his recent training load, his zones, what he'd been building toward. Tim didn't have to paste any of that. The conversation started from there.

It rebuilt the week around a sensible return — lighter structure, clear sessions, pacing guidance so he knew exactly what effort made sense coming back.

Tim's words: *"I came back without guessing, panicking, or overdoing it."*

That's the difference between an AI that knows your context and one that doesn't.

— Sam

P.S. Tim finished his backyard ultra.
