# The Self-Coached Cyclist's Playbook
## Training when your week never goes to plan

*Free guide from Sam Sutherland, founder of Streeka*

---

Most training plans are written for the week you hope to have.

Not the one where a meeting runs long, the kids are sick, sleep was bad, and Tuesday's interval session is staring at you from the calendar at 9pm like an accusation.

That gap — between the plan and the week — is where most of us quietly fall apart. Not because we lack commitment. Because the plan wasn't built for how our lives actually work.

This is the framework I use instead.

---

## Why plans fail (it's not you)

A training plan that can't survive disruption isn't a training plan. It's a wish list.

The problem is in the design. Most plans assume clean weeks — consistent sleep, no travel, no work blowouts, a body that cooperates. Stack three bad nights of sleep on top of a deadline week and the plan doesn't adapt. It just falls further behind. You miss a session. Then you feel guilty. Then you try to catch up. Then you're fatigued. Then you miss another one.

The plan didn't account for your life. That's a design flaw, not a character flaw.

---

## What a real coach does when the week falls apart

A good coach doesn't try to rescue everything. They ask one question:

*What's the most important thing to protect this week?*

Not "how do we fit it all in" — that's gone. "Of what's left, what moves the needle most?"

That reframe changes everything. You go from behind-and-catching-up to choosing-and-executing. One good session in a hard week is a win. Three mediocre sessions squeezed into the gaps is just fatigue.

---

## The 3-move adjustment: when a single session drops

When one session falls out of the week, you have three options. Pick one.

**1. Move it.** Does it fit anywhere else without creating back-to-back hard efforts? If yes, move it. If not, go to option 2.

**2. Shorten it.** A 30-minute version of most sessions still delivers most of the training signal. Half a session beats no session, and it doesn't wreck the rest of the week.

**3. Drop it.** Not every session has equal weight. Know which ones do. An endurance base ride is more replaceable than a threshold interval set at a critical phase of the season. When you have to drop something, drop the less important one.

The rule: never cram a dropped session into the day before a harder one. The fatigue compounds and you underperform on the session that actually matters.

---

## When the whole week blows up

Sometimes it's not one session. It's the whole week.

Work, travel, illness, a family situation. It happens. No training plan written on a clean Sunday accounts for it.

When a week blows up: pick one session, do it, call the week a win.

One session keeps the habit alive and the fitness loss minimal. Trying to rescue four sessions from a one-session week means doing all four at 60% — which produces less training adaptation than one honest effort, and costs more in recovery.

Let the week go. Do the one thing. Move on.

---

## Push through or cut load?

Two signals together tell you to cut load rather than push:

- Sleep has been poor for three or more consecutive nights (not one bad night — three)
- Effort feels high but power or pace is noticeably down

If both are true: cut load. A hard session on a depleted body doesn't create fitness. It creates injury risk and a recovery debt that costs you next week too.

If only one is true: start the session. Shorten the warm-up. See how the first ten minutes feel. You'll know.

---

## The thing that actually separates riders who improve

It's not the ones who execute perfectly. It's the ones who stay directionally consistent when things go wrong.

A messy week handled well beats a perfect week followed by two weeks of guilt-induced inactivity. The goal isn't perfection. The goal is to still be training in December — and in March, and in the year after that.

Every decision about a disrupted week should serve that goal. Not the plan on the page. The actual goal.
